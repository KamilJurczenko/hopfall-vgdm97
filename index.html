What is Hopfall?

💡 The Core Concept

🐸

TAME

Defeat corrupted frogs and win them over for future battles.

🎒

BUILD

Equip items, combine abilities, and discover team synergies.

♟️

ADAPT

Read enemy formations, reposition, and survive one battle at a time.

⚙️ Core Gameplay Loop

  • Scout the encounter — read the enemy board and identify the threat
  • Position your squad — front line, back line, and matchup decisions matter
  • Let the autobattle play out — then learn what worked and what failed
  • Tame new frogs — turn defeated enemies into future team members
  • Equip and optimize — use items and synergies to shape the next fight

A good run is a chain of smart pivots: one recruit, one item, or one formation change can redefine the build.

🧠 What Makes the Loop Interesting

Recruitment Decisions

Every defeated frog is a strategic question: raw power, synergy value, or bench depth?

Formation Counterplay

Autobattles stay active because the important choice happens before combat starts.

Synergy Discovery

The fun spike comes from seeing a strange frog-item combination suddenly make sense.

Roguelite Variety

Run identity changes through recruits, encounters, items, and the order you discover them.

🗺️ Run Structure

"The run should feel like an expedition: early discovery, mid-run commitment, late-game mastery checks."

Opening Battles: teach the basics of positioning, roles, and taming value.

Biome 1: establish your first real team identity and item priorities.

Biome 2: stronger enemy formations push you to commit, pivot, or fall behind.

Biome 3 + Bosses: the final tests for synergy quality, item economy, and adaptation.

🛠️ Building It as a Solo Dev

Design Priorities

Readable Battles Strong Recruitment Choices Run Variety

Production Reality

Scope Discipline System Reuse Content Efficiency

Core Question

How do I make each new frog feel like a real strategic option, not just another stat line?

Why This Genre Fits Solo Dev

High System Depth Lower Animation Burden Replayable Content

What Matters Most

If the audience can instantly understand who is winning and why, the autobattler becomes engaging instead of passive.

Solo Dev Leverage

One good taming system creates content, replayability, and a memorable hook at the same time.

🧱 Challenges We Faced

Problem: Autobattles can feel passive if the player does not understand what caused the outcome.

→ The solution is stronger readability: clearer unit roles, board states, and combat feedback.

Problem: Taming has to feel exciting without making progression feel random or trivial.

→ New recruits need distinct strategic value so each choice changes the run in a visible way.

Problem: With a solo-dev scope, content must scale without requiring bespoke logic everywhere.

→ Systems that combine well are more valuable than isolated features.

🧪 What Playtests Need to Confirm

1

Do players immediately understand that defeated frogs can become allies?

2

Can players explain why they won or lost a battle after it ends?

3

Does a new recruit feel like a meaningful choice instead of automatic value?

  • If battles are readable, players stay engaged even when combat is automated.
  • If taming choices feel distinct, the run starts telling its own story.

📚 Lessons Learned

  • Lead with the fantasy. "Tame the frogs you defeat" explains the game faster than genre labels do.
  • System depth beats feature count. One strong taming-and-synergy loop creates more value than many disconnected mechanics.
  • Readability is design. In an autobattler, clarity is not polish. It is the core of the player experience.
  • Solo-dev scope matters. Reusable systems are what make ambitious strategy games feasible to build alone.
